THE FRUSTRATION

Why Many Fonts
Fail on Cricut

Most display fonts are drawn for posters and screens, not for cutting machines. Strokes get too thin for vinyl or HTV, counters close up when you shrink text, and delicate curls turn into tiny islands your blade has to fight through.

You see this every time a “cute” script turns names into a knot, or when a long quote becomes unreadable once you size it down.

The letters may technically cut, but weeding them is slow and unforgiving, and any small mistake forces you to start over.

Questions Before You Start Cutting

Q. Are these fonts safe for small text on labels and tags?

A lot depends on the material and your blade, but these fonts were chosen because their strokes do not collapse immediately at small sizes. For very tiny text, test a short word first and adjust size until weeding feels comfortable.

Q. Can I use these fonts on products I sell?

Yes, these fonts include a commercial license for finished physical products and many printables. Always check the license page for details on logos or large‑scale redistribution of font files.

Q. What is the difference between Cricut Fonts and system fonts?

Cricut Fonts live inside Design Space and work on any device you sign into with your account. System fonts (like these) are installed on your computer and show up under the “System” tab in Design Space.

Q. Will these fonts work with other cutting machines?

Yes, if your machine accepts standard font files like OTF or TTF. You can use them with Silhouette, Brother and other cutters once the fonts are installed on the device or computer that runs their software.

Q. What is the best way to test a new font?

Start with a short word in the material you plan to use most. Cut it at the size you need, weed it slowly and check whether any strokes feel too fragile, then adjust size or switch to a sturdier font from this list.

Ready to Try These Fonts on Your Own Projects?

Pick one project you already have in mind — a label set, a gift, a shirt, a card — and test it with one or two fonts from this page first. Once you see how they cut, you can build a small library of Cricut‑ready fonts and stop guessing which designs will survive the mat.
